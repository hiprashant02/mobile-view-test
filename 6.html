1.6 The Abolition of Slavery

1.6.1 The Triangular Slave Trade

  • One of the most revolutionary social reforms of the Jacobin regime was the abolition of slavery in the French colonies.
  • The colonies in the Caribbean – Martinique, Guadeloupe, and San Domingo – were important suppliers of commodities like sugar, coffee, and indigo.
  • These products were cultivated using slave labor.
  • France was involved in the Triangular Slave Trade: This trade connected Europe, Africa, and the Americas. French merchants transported slaves from Africa to the American colonies.

1.6.2 The Conditions of Slaves

  • Slaves were treated as the property of their masters and had no personal rights.
  • They were subjected to extreme cruelty and harsh working conditions on plantations.
  • There was a constant demand for more slaves due to high mortality rates and brutal working conditions.

1.6.3 The Abolition Movement

  • The Convention, led by the Jacobins, passed laws to free the slaves in the French colonies in 1794.
  • This decision was influenced by the growing abolitionist movement and the principles of equality and liberty promoted by the revolution.
  • However, the abolition of slavery did not last long. Napoleon reintroduced slavery in 1802 to gain support from plantation owners.

1.6.4 Final Abolition of Slavery

  • Slavery was finally abolished in French colonies in 1848.
  • This marked a significant step towards human rights and social justice.
