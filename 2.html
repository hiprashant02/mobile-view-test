1.2 French Society During the Late Eighteenth Century

1.2.1 The Three Estates

  • French society in the late eighteenth century was divided into three estates.
  • The First Estate: Comprised of the clergy. They enjoyed certain privileges, such as exemption from paying taxes.
  • The Second Estate: Made up of the nobility. They also enjoyed privileges, including exemption from taxes and access to high offices.
  • The Third Estate: Included the common people. This estate was further divided into three groups:
    • Peasants: Made up about 90% of the population but owned only a small portion of land. They were burdened with taxes and feudal dues.
    • Workers: Included artisans, servants, and laborers. They lived in crowded cities and faced tough working conditions.
    • Middle Class: Consisted of professionals such as lawyers, doctors, and merchants. They were well-educated and often frustrated by their lack of social mobility.

1.2.2 The Struggle to Survive

  • The population of France increased from 23 million in 1715 to 28 million in 1789.
  • This led to a rapid increase in the demand for food grains.
  • Production of grains could not keep up with the demand, resulting in increased prices of bread, the staple diet of the majority.
  • Most workers were employed as laborers in workshops, and their wages did not keep pace with the rising prices.
  • Subsistence Crisis: This economic hardship led to widespread discontent among the common people. A subsistence crisis occurs when basic means of livelihood are endangered, leading to severe hunger and poverty.

1.2.3 Growing Middle Class

  • The eighteenth century witnessed the emergence of a new social group, the middle class.
  • They were prosperous and educated individuals, including merchants, manufacturers, and professionals.
  • The middle class believed in ideas of social equality and justice, inspired by philosophers such as John Locke and Jean-Jacques Rousseau.
  • They played a significant role in the revolution by demanding an end to privileges based on birth.
